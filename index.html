Shaken Baby Syndrome

Investigating The Abusive Head Trauma Controversy

Edited by

Keith A. Findley, Cyrille Rossant, Kana Sasakura, Leila Schneps, Waney Squier, Knut Wester.

The first collaborative, multidisciplinary academic textbook to tackle the scientific reliability of medical determinations of abusive head injuries in infants, a highly controversial subject at the intersection of medicine, science, and law.

Key Points

Learn from the top worldwide experts about the science behind medical determinations of shaken baby syndrome (SBS) / abusive head trauma (AHT).

Provides in-depth, evidence-based analyses of shaken baby syndrome from a multidisciplinary and global team of 32 authors in 26 chapters.
Covers the full range of topics and issues, including reviews of radiological and neuropathological findings in alleged SBS cases, some of their known medical causes, biomechanical and epidemiological aspects, police interrogation techniques and false confessions, cognitive biases among medical experts, evidence standards in courts, and the challenges of overturning wrongful convictions.
Investigates the claim that certain intracranial findings of unknown origin can alone (with no or minimal evidence of external trauma) be used as an absolute proof that shaking has occurred and that this is an intentional and hence abusive act.

Co-editors

In SBS/AHT, the tension at the interface of law and science is starkly evident. Science is never settled, it continually evolves, replacing old truths with new, better-informed truths. The law demands swift and definitive answers. It does not leave much room for incorporating new knowledge.

is Professor of Law at the University of Wisconsin, and former president of the Innocence Network.

French national SBS diagnosis guidelines go well beyond the standards in other countries in terms of claiming ‘certainty’ on the sole basis of one or two components of the ‘triad’. This diagnostic process contradicts the recent international ‘consensus statement’ denying the existence of ‘near-mechanical determination based on the “triad”’.

Cyrille Rossant

is a neuroscience researcher and software engineer at the Institute of Neurology, University College London.

The SBS/AHT hypothesis has become a controversial topic of debate in Japan, and a number of court decisions in criminal cases since 2018 have questioned the hypothesis or diagnoses based on it. A full review of cases that were convicted in the past is essential.

Kana Sasakura

is a professor of criminal procedure law at Konan University, Kobe.

There are multiple published cases of children presenting with subdural and retinal hemorrhage after witnessed short falls. Yet the claim that a short fall must be rejected as an explanation for these lesions is regularly cited in court as proof that a parent or caretaker is lying about the history of a child's accident.

Leila Schneps

is Professor of Mathematics working at Sorbonne University, Paris.

The explanations of the shaking hypothesis currently offered mainly misunderstand infant neuropathology. Others rely on confessions and consensus which rank low in the hierarchy of scientific evidence. A better understanding of pathology and science should lead to more accurate diagnosis and fairer assessment of cases in the courts.

Waney Squier

is a retired paediatric neuropathologist, formerly John Radcliffe Hospital, Oxford, UK.

I am quite convinced that benign external hydrocephalus is very often misdiagnosed as abusive head trauma, thus causing a large proportion of false accusations of child abuse.

Knut Wester

is a neurosurgeon, and Professor Emeritus in the Department of Clinical Medicine at the University of Bergen, Norway.

The topic

Shaken Baby Syndrome (SBS), also known as Abusive Head Trauma (AHT), refers not only to abusive acts where head injuries are inflicted to young children, but also to a controversial medical hypothesis: that certain findings found in infants (blood around the brain and at the back of the eyes) are almost always caused by violent trauma.

For the past decades, a growing body of scientific studies in neuropathology, neurology, biomechanics, statistics, and psychology has cast doubt on the validity of this idea. It is now known that the same findings may be caused by accidental short falls and a wide range of medical conditions. Nevertheless, mischaracterized determinations of child abuse and wrongful convictions continue to this day.

Bringing together contributions from a multidisciplinary expert panel of 32 world-leading professionals, this landmark book explains the scientific evidence challenging automatic determinations of SBS/AHT and advances efforts to evaluate how deaths and serious brain injuries in infants should be analysed and investigated.
